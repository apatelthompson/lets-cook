# Module 0: The Why

**Goal:** The student understands why this workshop exists and what they're actually learning — not just "how to code" but how to speak the language of labor.
**Time:** ~10 minutes (often delivered by the human facilitator live, before Claude takes over)
**Note for Claude:** If the student joins without having heard this framing, deliver it conversationally before starting Module 1. Don't read it verbatim — make it a real conversation.

---

## The Opening Frame

Start with the cooking analogy — it's disarming and honest:

> "We're going to learn how to cook today. Some really basic things. Nothing fancy.
>
> To do that, we first need to understand the kitchen — the appliances, the tools, how things are laid out, what's safe to do and what's hard to undo.
>
> Then we're going to understand what a good end product looks like. What are the components. How do they fit together. What makes something work versus what makes something break.
>
> Then we're going to read a recipe and follow the steps. And we're going to make mistakes — and we're going to learn which mistakes are just modifications, and which ones are hard to come back from.
>
> By the end of today, you will have cooked something. A real thing. That anyone in the world can visit."

---

## The Deeper Why

After the cooking frame lands, go here:

**Why does this matter? Why cooking?**

Not everyone needs to become a chef. But everyone needs to be able to feed themselves.

Right now, there's a shift happening in how work gets done. AI isn't just a tool — it's increasingly labor. It's doing things that used to require humans: writing, analyzing, designing, coding, researching.

And the way you direct that labor — the way you tell it what to do, how to do it, what to prioritize — is through code.

Not necessarily complex code. Not necessarily becoming a software engineer. But being able to speak the language at all.

**Use the lingua franca analogy:**

Think about how many people in the world learn English as a second language. Not because they love English. Not because England is culturally dominant. But because it became the shared language — the one you need to participate in global business, science, travel, conversation.

A percentage of people born in non-English-speaking countries learn English because if you don't, you're not in the room where conversations happen.

Code is becoming that language for work.

If you don't speak it — even a little — you're going to find yourself increasingly on the outside of conversations about how work gets done. What work even is. Whether you want a machine to do something, or whether you want a person to. What's worth automating and what isn't.

**The honest version:**

For people over 40, it's debatable whether learning to code is strictly necessary. The world will have adapted more by the time it fully matters. But even then — probably yes, it matters.

For people under 40? Not debatable. This is the language of the world you're going to be working in. You need to at least know how to order off the menu.

---

## What "Learning to Cook" Actually Means Here

Be specific about what they're not doing:

- We're not becoming software engineers today.
- We're not learning everything.
- We're not going to memorize syntax or become fluent by the end of this workshop.

What we are doing:

- Getting in the kitchen for real. Touching the tools.
- Building something that actually works and actually goes on the internet.
- Getting comfortable enough that the next time you're in a conversation about software, a product, an AI tool — you're in the room. You have context. You can ask the right questions.
- And having the confidence to know: if I needed to build something, I know how to start.

---

## Transition to Module 1

End the opening with something like:

> "Alright. First thing we do in any kitchen — we set it up. We arrange our tools. We make it feel like ours.
>
> Let's go look at your kitchen."

Then move to Module 1.

---

## If They Push Back ("I'm too old for this" / "I'm not a tech person")

Respond to this directly, without dismissing it:

> "I hear that. And I'm not going to tell you it's easy, because it's not always easy. But 'not a tech person' is less a fixed trait and more a story we tell ourselves.
>
> You're not here to become a tech person. You're here to learn to cook. Everyone can learn to cook. Let's just see what happens."

Then move on. Don't over-argue it. Let the work prove the point.
